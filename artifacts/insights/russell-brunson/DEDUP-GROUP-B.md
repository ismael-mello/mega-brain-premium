## MENTAL_MODELS (MM)

- ID: MM-RB-100
  - Name: The Attractive Character Model
  - Description: The concept that if you create and present yourself in a certain way, people will be attracted to you -- just like attraction in dating. The laws of attraction for customers/clients are the same as personal attraction. Learned from John Allinus.
  - Application: When building any personal brand or expert business -- defines how to present yourself.
  - Module_Sources: M03

- ID: MM-RB-101
  - Name: Guru On The Mountain
  - Description: Dan Kennedy concept: You are the guru on the mountain. People want to ascend to you, but they won't unless they know you once walked in their shoes. Your backstory bridges the gap between "that person is unreachable" and "I can follow them." In M05, this becomes the Mountain and Valley Positioning: as an expert, people see you up on the mountain of achievement. To create empathy and connection, you must come back DOWN to where the audience is.
  - Application: When crafting your origin story and positioning -- must show you once were where they are now.
  - Module_Sources: M03, M05

- ID: MM-RB-102
  - Name: The Oprah Effect / Spin-Off Brand Model
  - Description: When you have a massive following and attractive character, anything you recommend sells explosively. Companies went bankrupt because they couldn't handle the orders from a single Oprah mention. Long-term vision: build platform, then spin off brands (Dr. Phil, Dr. Oz), take a cut of every spin-off, create your own network.
  - Application: Long-term platform vision -- build audience first, then leverage into multiple revenue streams.
  - Module_Sources: M03

- ID: MM-RB-103
  - Name: Soap Opera = Relationship Builder
  - Description: The first emails someone receives after opting in determine whether they stay or leave forever. SOAP Opera Sequences bond the new subscriber to the attractive character through serialized storytelling with hooks that pull readers to the next email.
  - Application: First 3+ days after opt-in -- the critical relationship-building window.
  - Module_Sources: M03

- ID: MM-RB-104
  - Name: Seinfeld = Perpetual Engagement Engine
  - Description: After the initial relationship is built via SOAP Opera Sequence, daily "show about nothing" emails keep the audience engaged forever. Tell stories about your daily life, then weave them back to whatever you're selling.
  - Application: Ongoing daily email strategy after the SOAP Opera Sequence completes.
  - Module_Sources: M03

- ID: MM-RB-105
  - Name: Two Ways To Build The Tallest Building

  - Module_Sources: M03

- ID: MM-RB-106

  - Description: Russell went from monthly to twice monthly to weekly to daily emails, and each increase more than doubled his income. Under-emailing is the default mistake. Some marketers email 2-6 times per day profitably.

- ID: MM-RB-110
  - Name: Traffic Temperature Model
  - Description: Three temperatures of traffic based on Gene Schwartz's awareness levels: HOT = product aware, know you and want to buy; WARM = solution aware, know the category but not you; COLD = problem aware or unaware, only know they have a pain. The page/funnel you build must match the temperature of traffic you are driving.

  - Module_Sources: M04

- ID: MM-RB-117
  - Name: The Itch Has Been Scratched
  - Description: When someone buys a product on a specific topic, in their mind that problem is solved even though they haven't consumed it yet. Upselling more of the same topic fails because "the itch is scratched." You must sell the NEXT STEP in the process, not more of the same.
  - Application: When sequencing upsells -- always sell the next logical step, never more of the same.
  - Module_Sources: M04

- ID: MM-RB-118
  - Name: Open Loop vs Closed Loop Selling
  - Description: Saying "your order is complete, thanks" closes the mental loop and kills upsell conversions. Saying "wait, your order is not yet complete" keeps the loop open and dramatically increases conversion on the next offer.
  - Application: When designing post-purchase flow -- never close the loop until all upsells are presented.
  - Module_Sources: M04

- ID: MM-RB-119
  - Name: Funnel Hacking As Competitive Intelligence
  - Description: Treat competitor funnels as free market research: buy their products, track their email sequences, screenshot every page, note every price point. You get years of their testing and optimization for free in a week.
  - Application: Before building any funnel -- hack 3-5 competitors first.
  - Module_Sources: M04

- ID: MM-RB-120
  - Name: Comfort Zone Sales Environment Theory
  - Description: People behind computers are in their comfort zone and resistant to high-ticket sales. Changing the selling environment -- events, phone calls, in-person -- removes comfort zone resistance and enables $25K-$100K+ sales that are "pretty much impossible" online.
  - Application: When selling high-ticket ($15K+) -- change the environment from online to phone/event/in-person.
  - Module_Sources: M04

- ID: MM-RB-121
  - Name: The Big Domino Model
  - Description: Every sales message has ONE belief that, if established, causes all objections and concerns to become irrelevant and forces the prospect to buy. Structure: "If I can make them believe that [new opportunity] is the key to [what they desire most] and is only attainable through [my specific vehicle], then all objections become irrelevant and they have to give me money."
  - Application: When crafting any sales message -- identify the single belief that must be created.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-122
  - Name: Blue Ocean vs Red Ocean Selling
  - Description: If your Big Domino statement is not a logically true statement -- if the customer can get the same result from 18,000 other products -- you are in a red ocean. The Big Domino must place you in a blue ocean where your vehicle is the ONLY way to get the result.
  - Application: When testing your Big Domino statement -- if it's not a blue ocean statement, reposition.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-123
  - Name: Three False Belief Categories
  - Description: The three types of false beliefs that prop up the Big Domino and prevent it from falling: 1) False beliefs about the VEHICLE -- "I don't believe funnels work"; 2) False beliefs about THEMSELVES (internal) -- "I'm not techie enough"; 3) False beliefs about EXTERNAL things -- "I don't know how to drive traffic."
  - Application: When identifying objections -- categorize every objection into Vehicle, Internal, or External.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-124
  - Name: Experience -> Story -> Belief Chain
  - Description: Every belief a person holds was created through a specific chain: they had an EXPERIENCE, from that experience they created a STORY they tell themselves, and that story became a BELIEF pattern. To change the belief, you must replace their story with a better one using an Epiphany Bridge story.
  - Application: When trying to change any belief -- trace it back to the original experience/story, then replace with a new story.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-125
  - Name: Expert Layer vs Product Layer
  - Description: When selling a commodity product, making the Big Domino statement is nearly impossible because customers can buy the same thing elsewhere. Moving one layer back to the EXPERT level "uncommoditizes" the offering and makes the Big Domino attainable.
  - Application: When stuck in a commodity market -- sell the expert/method, not the product.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-126
  - Name: The Zeigarnik Effect in Sales
  - Description: A waiter remembers an order perfectly until it is completed, then forgets it immediately. Until a cognitive loop is closed, the brain cannot let go of it. Use this by opening curiosity loops in headlines, emails, and teasers that can only be closed by attending the presentation.
  - Application: When writing headlines, subject lines, and teasers -- open loops that demand closure.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-127
  - Name: Price Marinating
  - Description: Myron Golden's concept: introduce the price early and leave it visible throughout the entire presentation. First reaction is shock, but from that point forward, the rest of the presentation proves the value. By the time you close, the price has been "marinating" for the full 90 minutes.
  - Application: When structuring a webinar or presentation -- show the price early and let it marinate.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-128
  - Name: Story Stacking
  - Description: Telling multiple stories about the same objection or belief, one after another, each knocking it down from a different angle. Like dominoes tipping -- first they think "maybe" then "okay" then "I have no more excuses." Russell does this naturally when handling "I can't afford it" with Rich Dad Poor Dad story, then raking leaves story, then buddy financing story.
  - Application: When a single story doesn't fully break a belief -- stack multiple stories for the same objection.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-129
  - Name: Audience Maturity Levels
  - Description: The same offer may be a huge new opportunity for one audience and old news for another. Network marketers hearing about online business -- massive new opportunity. Internet marketers hearing about online business -- not new at all. The Big Domino must be calibrated to the audience's maturity level.
  - Application: When positioning your offer -- calibrate the "new opportunity" framing to your specific audience's awareness.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-130
  - Name: One-to-One vs One-to-Many Selling
  - Description: In one-on-one selling, you can tailor the story to the specific person's situation. In one-to-many, you must make the message inclusive so everyone feels it speaks to them. At the beginning, address different segments: "How many of you are just getting started? How many have a successful business?" -- so everyone feels it is for them.
  - Application: When presenting to groups -- use inclusive openings to prevent self-selection out.
  - Module_Sources: M05

- ID: MM-RB-131
  - Name: Story Inventory Model
  - Description: Dan Kennedy's concept: build a lifetime inventory of stories mapped to every objection you ever receive. Write them down, practice them, so you can pull them out instantly when needed. The more you practice telling them, the faster they pop out of your mind when you need them in the moment.
  - Application: Ongoing practice -- maintain and grow a written inventory of stories matched to objections.
  - Module_Sources: M05
